    Venus
    Venus, the second planet from the Sun and sixth-largest in our solar system, has a radius of about 6,051 kilometers, approximately 95% that of Earth, resulting in a surface gravity of roughly 90% compared to Earth's. It's the closest large body to Earth aside from the Moon, always visible near sunrise or sunset, and appears as the most brilliant planet in the sky. Venus is symbolized by ♀ and shares many similarities in size and mass with Earth. Venus is often considered Earth's twin because of its similar size, mass, and density. Initially, it was thought to be a wet, Earth-like world, but it is currently extremely hot and dry, unsuitable for life. Scientists study Venus to learn about its extreme conditions and to gain insights into environmental changes on Earth.When the opposition phenomenon with Earth occurs, the distance between them is about 38 million kilometers (about 24 million miles) to 41 million kilometers (about 25.5 million miles).This phenomenon repeats every 19 to 22 months

    3.Venusian Botanical Gardens: Step into the future of Venus through our climate-controlled domes. Experience heat-resistant plant species and Venusian soil simulation while learning from expert botanists and guides about the potential for terraforming this mysterious world
    Mars
    Mars, the fourth planet in our solar system, is known as the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance. It's about half the size of Earth but shares some Earth-like features like clouds, winds, a 24-hour day, and seasonal weather patterns. Mars has lower gravity (0.375 times that of Earth) and two moons, Phobos and Deimos. While once considered a potential habitat for life, Mars is now known for its barren, frozen landscape. It orbits the Sun at varying distances, with its closest approach during the opposition phenomenon, which repeats every 26 months. Mars also features Valles Marineris, the deepest canyon, and Olympus Mons, the highest volcano in the solar system.

    Jupiter
    Jupiter, the most massive planet in our solar system and fifth from the Sun, is a prominent celestial object in the night sky. Its surface gravity is 2.4 times that of Earth due to its gas composition. Jupiter, symbolized by ♃, is named after the Roman ruler of the gods. It's enormous, 11 times larger than Earth, with colorful cloud bands visible even with a small telescope. Jupiter has a narrow ring system and an impressive 92 known moons, including Europa, possibly hiding a warm-water ocean.The planet has an internal heat source, emits more energy than it receives from the Sun, and its hydrogen exists in a fluid metallic state due to extreme pressure. Jupiter boasts the solar system's strongest magnetic field and a massive magnetosphere. During the opposition phenomenon, when Earth and Jupiter are closest, they are about 365 million miles apart, repeating every 13 months.

    Uranus
    Uranus, the seventh planet from the Sun and the least massive of the solar system's giant planets, is mainly composed of gases, resulting in a surface gravity about 86% that of Earth. It appears as a blue-green point of light and was discovered in 1781, making it the first planet found in modern times. Uranus lacks a solid surface and gets its color from methane in its atmosphere. It has a unique 84-year orbit and is almost circular, with a nearly flat alignment compared to the solar system's plane. The opposition phenomenon, when Earth and Uranus are closest, occurs approximately every 12 months, at a distance of about 2.9 billion kilometers.
